## Context Checklist

Gather only what is needed to proceed:

- Sender: role, seniority, company, and whether the email comes from a founder, executive, SDR, or specialist.
- Offer: what they sell, who buys it, and what problem it solves.
- Proof: specific customer results, credible logos, benchmarks, or before/after evidence.
- Prospect: title, company type, size, market, and likely priority.
- Trigger: hiring, funding, expansion, tech stack, new initiative, regulation, content, job post, or visible operational signal.
- Goal: reply, referral, booked call, permission to send details, or qualification question.
- Constraints: geography, compliance requirements, tone, list source, and whether the campaign needs scale or high-touch personalization.

## Writing Standards

Write like a peer with a useful reason to reach out.

Rules:

- Lead with the prospect's world, not the sender's company.
- Connect personalization to the business problem.
- Keep every email focused on one idea and one ask.
- Use plain text.
- Prefer specific observations over broad claims.
- Keep proof short and relevant.
- Make the CTA easy to answer.

Length guidance:

| Email type | Target length |
| --- | ---: |
| First touch | 55-125 words |
| Follow-up | 40-100 words |
| Executive first touch | 35-85 words |
| Technical audience | 70-140 words if specificity is needed |

Strong first-touch structure:

```text
Subject: [short internal-looking subject]

Hi [First Name],

[Specific observation or trigger tied to their business problem.]

[One sentence on why this usually matters.]

[Brief proof or relevant contrast.]

[Low-friction question.]
```

## 3-Email Sequence

Default to exactly 3 emails unless the user says otherwise.

| Email | Day | Purpose | Angle |
| --- | ---: | --- | --- |
| 1 | 1 | Start a relevant conversation | Trigger/problem/proof/question |
| 2 | 4 | Add a new reason to care | Evidence, cost of inaction, or specific use case |
| 3 | 9 | Close the loop | Referral ask, priority check, or final low-friction question |

Follow-up rules:

- Add a new angle; do not say "just checking in."
- Make the follow-up understandable without reading the prior email.
- Do not stack CTAs.
- Keep the tone calm and unentitled.
- Stop if they reply, unsubscribe, or clearly are not a fit.

Email 3 can be a light breakup, but do not overdo drama. Good patterns:

- "Worth speaking with you, or better to ask someone else?"
- "Is this on your team's radar this quarter?"
- "If this belongs with someone else, who owns it?"

## Subject Lines

Goal: get a relevant open without looking like an ad.

Use:

- 1-4 words.
- Lowercase or normal sentence case.
- Specific trigger when useful.
- Internal-looking wording.

Patterns:

| Pattern | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Short question | `quick question` |
| Trigger | `sdr hiring` |
| Business object | `your outbound motion` |
| Company context | `[Company] expansion` |
| Referral, only if true | `[Name] suggested I ask` |

Avoid:

- Fake `Re:` or `Fwd:`.
- Emojis.
- All caps.
- Blog-post style numbers.
- The sender's company name unless there is existing recognition.
- Hype, urgency, or clickbait.

## Audience Calibration

| Audience | What to optimize for |
| --- | --- |
| CEO/founder | Short, strategic, direct, one clear question |
| CRO/VP Sales | Pipeline economics, speed, conversion, team focus |
| CMO/VP Marketing | ICP, positioning, campaign efficiency, attribution |
| Director/Manager | Operational pain, practical next step, low friction |
| Technical buyer | Precision, integration details, credibility, no fluff |

The higher the seniority, the shorter the email should usually be.

## Critique Workflow

When reviewing a draft:

1. Identify the biggest issue first: relevance, clarity, proof, CTA, length, tone, deliverability, or personalization.
2. Flag exact lines that sound templated, inflated, or vendor-like.
3. Rewrite the email.
4. Explain only the changes that matter.
5. Suggest one test, not a pile of experiments.

Common rewrites:

- Replace "I hope this email finds you well" with a relevant observation.
- Replace "I'm reaching out because" with the reason itself.
- Replace feature lists with one business problem.
- Replace "Let me know if you're interested" with a direct question.
- Replace a 30-45 minute meeting ask with a lower-friction reply or 15-minute ask.

## Performance Diagnosis

Ask for open rate, reply rate, positive reply rate, bounce rate, spam placement, send volume, audience, and the current emails.

Use this diagnosis map:

| Signal | Likely issue | Fix |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Low open rate | Subject line, sender reputation, deliverability, targeting | Test subject/sender, check placement, narrow ICP |
| Opens but no replies | Body relevance, proof, CTA, weak trigger | Rewrite opener, sharpen problem, lower CTA friction |
| Replies but wrong people | ICP/list quality or referral ask | Rebuild segment and ask for owner |
| High bounces or bounce rate above 3% | List quality or verification | Pause or slow sending, verify leads, clean the list |
| Spam placement rising | Infrastructure or content risk | Slow down, check DNS, simplify copy |
| Positive replies but low booked calls | Handoff/CTA/friction | Shorter next step, faster replies, clearer ask |

Reply handling standard:

- Answer human replies in 5 minutes or less.
- Ideal response time is 1 minute or less.
- If positive replies are not being answered quickly, fix response ops before increasing volume.

## Patterns to Avoid

Avoid these unless the user explicitly asks for a risky example:

- "I hope this email finds you well."
- "My name is..."
- "I wanted to reach out because..."
- "Just checking in" or "circling back."
- Long company introductions.
- Feature dumps.
- Fake familiarity.
- Unrelated personalization.
- Multiple CTAs.
- Calendar links in the first email as the only ask.
- Big claims without proof.
- HTML-heavy templates, banners, logos, buttons, and tracking-heavy formatting.
