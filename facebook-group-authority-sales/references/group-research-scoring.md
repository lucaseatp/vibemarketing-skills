# Group Research and Scoring

Use this reference to find relevant groups and rank them from 0 to 10.

## Group Types

Start with communities where the ideal customer actually participates, not peer groups for the user's profession. For a local service provider, for example, neighborhoods, residential communities, and resident groups usually make more sense than groups of other providers.

Useful group types:

- Local communities: city, neighborhood, residential community, homeowners or residents association, local residents, parents, and local recommendation groups.
- Interest communities: hobbies, lifestyle, health, pets, events, local shopping, families, students, and people with a specific routine.
- Life-stage communities: moving to a city, newcomers, new parents, homeowners, retirees, college students, and local entrepreneurs.
- Problem-based communities: groups where the problem the user solves appears naturally, without turning the conversation into classifieds.
- Civic and event communities: fairs, schools, sports, volunteering, fundraising, charity events, and local initiatives.

## Search Keywords

Combine location, community, lifestyle, problem, and the audience's native language. Adapt the terms to the user's niche.

Agnostic models:

- `[city] locals`
- `[city] community`
- `[city] residents`
- `[neighborhood]`
- `[neighborhood] neighbors`
- `[residential community] residents`
- `[city] recommendations`
- `[city] events`
- `[city] families`
- `[city] moms`
- `[city] dads`
- `[city] newcomers`
- `[city] small business`
- `[city] buy sell trade`
- `[problem the user solves] [city]`
- `[audience interest] [city]`
- `[audience life stage] [city]`

For each niche, generate specific keywords based on the ideal customer. Example: for residential services, prioritize neighborhoods, residential communities, residents, and local recommendations; for a children's brand, prioritize parents, schools, events, and family activities; for fitness, prioritize running, gyms, wellness, and local events.

## 0-10 Score

Calculate the final score using three dimensions:

- Group size: number of people in the group.
- Contextual relevance: how much the group makes sense for the user, offer, region, and real authority.
- Engagement: number of recent posts with comments, reactions, and relevant conversations.

When possible, record the observed window, for example: last 7 days, last 30 days, or posts visible on the first screen. Do not bypass privacy or Facebook rules; if the group is private or the data is not visible, ask the user for screenshots, numbers, or a manual summary.

Do not treat all engagement as good engagement. Direct recommendation request posts that collect comments from vendors self-promoting should carry little strategic weight because they put the user in explicit competition and do not build natural authority.

Use this scale:

- 0-2: weak, irrelevant, inactive, or risky group.
- 3-4: possible, but low priority.
- 5-6: useful, with some fit.
- 7-8: good group to test presence.
- 9-10: priority group, highly relevant and active.

## Recommended Formula

Use simple weights:

- 30% group size.
- 40% contextual relevance.
- 30% recent engagement.

Also record a short rationale. Example:

```text
Score: 8/10
Size: 7/10
Relevance: 9/10
Engagement: 8/10
Rationale: active local community, frequent resident conversations, and recurring problems related to the user's offer.
```

If the group has high activity but most of it comes from self-promotion in the comments, reduce the strategic engagement score.

## Expected Output

Deliver a table with:

- Group name.
- URL.
- Type.
- Location.
- Number of members.
- Number of posts with engagement in the observed window.
- Qualitative engagement signals.
- Relevance to the user.
- Final score.
- Priority: high, medium, or low.
- Next action: join, observe, comment, request rules, avoid.
