# Extraction And Synthesis

Use this guide when source material is messy, sparse, spread across files, or originally structured as broader business foundation docs. Lack of perfect source material should not block creation of `marketing-context.md`.

## Source Priority

Prioritize sources in this order:

1. Explicit user instruction in the current conversation.
2. Existing `marketing-context.md`.
3. Current product, pricing, checkout, contract, or app files.
4. Current website, landing page, onboarding, sales, or campaign files.
5. Existing business foundation docs.
6. Research notes, interviews, transcripts, call summaries, and strategy notes.
7. Discovery answers from the user.
8. Careful inference from surrounding context.

When sources conflict, prefer newer and more operational sources for facts. Preserve conflicts as open questions when unsure.

## Sparse-Context Workflow

When the user has no formal docs:

1. Search for obvious context first: website copy, README, app names, package names, pricing files, landing pages, product screens, campaign drafts, and any existing `marketing-context.md`.
2. Ask a focused discovery interview only for missing high-value facts.
3. Draft the file with three confidence levels visible: confirmed facts, informed assumptions, and open questions.
4. Make the context useful for immediate marketing work even if not every section is complete.
5. Put the most important missing details in `Assumptions And Open Questions` with a practical validation path.

Do not tell the user the skill cannot proceed because the full business foundation document set is missing.

## Mapping From Business Foundation Docs

If the workspace has broader foundation docs, extract them into `marketing-context.md` like this:

| Source doc | Pull into marketing context |
| --- | --- |
| `briefing-geral.md` | Business snapshot, category, opportunity, current priorities, risks |
| `briefing-marketing.md` | Audience, pains, desires, objections, channels, campaign angles, CTAs |
| `perfil-cliente-ideal.md` or `ideal-customer-profile.md` | ICP, segments, jobs, triggers, buying criteria, disqualifiers |
| `proposta-de-valor-posicionamento.md` or `value-proposition-positioning.md` | Positioning, value proposition, differentiators, proof, messages |
| `branding-foundation.md` | Voice, tone, brand beliefs, vocabulary, message pillars, things to avoid |
| `planos-e-precos.md` or `plans-pricing.md` | Offer structure, plans, pricing, limits, source-of-truth gaps |
| `business-blueprint.md` | Business model, delivery model, channels, metrics, strategic assumptions |
| `assumptions-and-open-questions.md` | Assumptions, unanswered questions, evidence gaps, validation ideas |

| `quem-somos.md` or `about.md` | Founder narrative, mission, values, institutional language |

## Synthesis Rules

Turn raw notes into marketing-useful decisions:

- Convert broad audience descriptions into ICP and disqualifiers.
- Convert feature lists into problems solved, outcomes, and proof.
- Convert founder beliefs into brand point of view and voice.
- Convert pricing notes into offer, package, plan, and conversion-path facts.
- Convert risks into guardrails and open questions.
- Convert research quotes into customer language when they are real quotes or close paraphrases.

## Confidence Labels

Use simple labels:

- **Confirmed**: directly supported by a current source or explicit user statement.
- **Assumption**: plausible but not verified.
- **Hypothesis**: a strategic belief that should be tested.
- **Open question**: missing or conflicting information.

Do not over-label every sentence. Label sections, tables, or risky statements where confidence matters.

## Business Design Lenses

Use these lenses to improve the context without bloating it:

- **Desirability**: do customers care enough, and what emotional or functional need is strongest?
- **Viability**: can the business capture value through the offer, pricing, and channels?
- **Feasibility**: can the team deliver the promise with current capacity?
- **Distinctiveness**: what makes the offer hard to replace with a generic alternative?
- **Evidence**: what has actually been observed, sold, shipped, tested, or heard from customers?

When evidence is weak, write the best current hypothesis and add a validation question.

## Validation Questions

Use these questions to fill gaps:

- What is the business and what does it sell?
- Who is the best-fit customer?
- What problem does the customer urgently want solved?
- What event or trigger makes the customer act now?
- What would they use instead?
- Why would they trust this business?
- What result can be claimed safely?
- Who is excluded from the ICP?
- What price, package, or CTA is actually current?
- What channel has evidence and what channel is only a bet?
- What claim could create legal, compliance, or trust risk?
- What one experiment would increase confidence fastest?

If time is limited, prioritize the first six questions. A useful first version beats an empty perfect template.

## Final Checks

Before finishing:

- Confirm the file path.
- Search for `[TODO]`, `[placeholder]`, fake names, and copied examples.
- Check pricing, claims, and customer results for source support.
- Make sure `marketing-context.md` can be read quickly by another marketing skill.
- Ensure open questions are specific enough to drive the next conversation.
